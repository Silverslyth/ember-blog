## What is Ember?

Ember is a public archive of useful knowledge. It exists to document solutions, preserve technical research, and organize information so that people can find clear answers — without wading through fragmented forums, outdated wikis, or pages buried under ads and tracking scripts.

This is not a blog in the traditional sense. There are no hot takes, no engagement metrics, no newsletter pop-ups. Ember is closer to a research notebook made public: a structured, searchable space where workflows, software discoveries, and documentation live together. Every guide you find here was written because someone — usually the author — asked a question, investigated the answer thoroughly, and took the time to write it down properly.

## Why This Site Exists

Most technical knowledge online degrades over time. It is scattered across platforms that change their URLs, repeated in a dozen slightly different versions, or written once and abandoned. You find a solution on Reddit from four years ago — the link is dead. A guide on Medium is now behind a paywall. A GitHub issue has the fix buried in a collapsed thread, six comments deep.

The purpose of Ember is to consolidate what works. Every guide, configuration, and workflow documented here has been tested and verified. The goal is simple: document it once, clearly, so nobody has to rediscover it from scratch.

## What You'll Find Here

The archive covers:

- **Linux** — system configuration, package management, desktop environments, terminal workflows, and kernel-level workflows
- **Streaming ecosystems** — Nuvio, Stremio, TorBox, debrid services, addon configuration, and cross-platform streaming setups
- **Privacy and security** — practical privacy tools, threat modelling for everyday users, and self-hosted alternatives to managed services
- **Automation** — shell scripting, cron-based workflows, task scheduling, and system maintenance
- **Self-hosting** — running your own services, Docker configurations, reverse proxies, and home lab setups
- **Software** — tool evaluations, comparison guides, and configuration references for open-source applications
- **Research** — investigation notes, deep dives into specific topics, and curated reference archives
- **Technical workflows** — end-to-end procedures for solving real problems, tested and reproducible

The common thread: everything here answers a question someone investigated thoroughly and took the time to document.

## Philosophy

- **Learn deeply.** Surface-level tutorials produce surface-level understanding. The guides here go deep enough that you can adapt the knowledge — not just copy-paste the commands.
- **Document clearly.** If it took three hours to figure out, it should take the next person three minutes to read.
- **Share freely.** Useful knowledge belongs in the open. No paywalls, no registration walls, no tracking.
- **Build systems that outlast motivation.** Good automation and good documentation mean you don't have to remember everything yourself — the system remembers for you.
